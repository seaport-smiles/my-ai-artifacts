The Foundation

What is Orthognathic Surgery?

Orthognathic surgery is utilized principally for skeletodental syndromes that are beyond the control of conventional orthodontic procedures. While traditional orthodontics focuses mainly on tooth movement, surgical intervention adds another dimension by repositioning the facial bones while it moves the teeth.

"Achieving a favorable soft tissue change is often the primary goal of the treatment plan, maximizing both aesthetic and functional results."

Clinical Indications

Comparing Skeletal Discrepancies

Jaw misalignment generally falls into two primary categories. While orthodontic camouflage can sometimes mask mild cases, surgical correction addresses the underlying bone structure.

Class III (Prognathism)
  • Mandibular overgrowth
  • Midface/Maxillary deficiency
  • Concave facial profile
  • Illusion of overgrown lower jaw
Class II (Retrognathism)
  • Underdeveloped mandible
  • Maxillary vertical overdevelopment
  • Recessed lower jaw
  • Downward/Backward jaw rotation
Skeletal Divergence

Vertical Maxillary Excess & Deep Bite

Vertical maxillary excess occurs when the upper jaw overdevelops vertically, often resulting in a "long face" syndrome and an anterior open bite. Conversely, a deep bite "short face" syndrome occurs when the lower face height is disproportionately small.

Comprehensive Care

Asymmetry, Airway & The Chin

Orthognathic procedures go beyond simple bite correction. For patients with chin insufficiency, a sliding genioplasty can advance the chin independently of the jaw to dramatically improve the profile.

Additionally, for patients suffering from obstructive sleep apnea, advancing the jaws surgically can physically expand the airway, providing significant, long-term health benefits.
